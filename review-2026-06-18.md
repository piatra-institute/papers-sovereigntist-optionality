# Review: sovereigntist-optionality

## Verdict
**Accept with major revisions** at a venue that publishes conceptual/theoretical contributions; **reject** at a venue demanding an empirical or formal advance. The arithmetic reproduces exactly, the prose is genuinely good, and two ideas (the composition-artifact reading of the aggregate, and the option-vs-capture identification point) are real. But the single most serious problem is a gap between the paper's billing and its content: it advertises *option pricing* and leans on the real-options literature (McDonald–Siegel, Dixit–Pindyck, Trigeorgis) for authority, while the model is plain expected-value threshold-crossing with no option value of waiting, no irreversibility, no volatility — it abandons the very mechanism it cites. Several headline comparisons also conflate parameter changes with population changes, and the model's "negative result" is partly true by construction.

## Claim grounding
- "the leu broke past 5 to the euro and fell by about 3%, 10-year yields rose by roughly 60 basis points, a debt auction was cut back" → **supported.** Verified: Simion won round one 4 May 2025 with 40.96%; leu fell ~3% past 5.10/€; 10-yr yields +~60bp; Romania curbed its debt sale. Accurate, and explicitly flagged as motivating (non-calibrating).
- "Solved over a population of 60,000 firms, the model locates a state-dependence threshold $s^{*}=0.4229$" → **overstated.** $s^{*}$ is a closed-form root of two linear expected-value lines (`threshold_s`, m=0.55); it is independent of the 60,000-firm draw. The population locates the *shares*, not the threshold.
- "the access value at stake jumps to $0.1287$, more than 4 times the rising case" → **overstated as a discretion effect.** Verified arithmetically (0.1287/0.0282 = 4.6×), but the consolidated regime changes $p$, $\delta$, $\rho$ **and** the population weights (0.55/0.20/0.25 vs 0.40/0.40/0.20) at once. A richer state-dependent population alone raises access value; the paper attributes the jump to discretion without holding composition fixed.
- "The discretion parameter is what separates the three" → **not established by the three runs.** The sensitivity tornado (which *does* hold population fixed) supports $\delta$ being the top driver of $s^{*}$ — but that is a different quantity than the cross-regime access values, where four things move together.
- "Under capture, by construction, none unwinds … the two data-generating processes … now read $0.0678$ against zero" → **partly true by construction.** The capture DGP is not modeled; `capture_reversibility` is hard-set to 0.0. Only the optionality side (6.78%, value-retained 0.2263) is computed. The paper says "the model computes it"; it computes one side and stipulates the other.
- "$0.0678$ of a hedging cohort … unwinds" / "retained access value falls to $0.2263$ of its peak" → **supported but fragile.** Both reproduce exactly from `results.json`, but both depend entirely on the stipulated $p_{hi}=0.6 \to p_{lo}=0.25$ drop and the population draw; presented as robust predictions, they are single points.
- "the larger share of subsidy spending went to press and propaganda" → **mildly overstated.** Provenance says ~48% — a plurality, not "the larger share" if read as a majority.
- Sensitivity values (Δ$\delta$=0.234, $\rho$=0.211, a=0.208, κ=0.191, φ=0.053) and all §4/§5 firm and share figures → **fully supported**, match the JSON to the decimal.

## Argument
- **§1, §3 — the framing oversells the formalism (most serious).** The paper opens on the real-options canon and says "the decision rule is not net present value but a threshold the expected return must clear before the option to wait is surrendered." The model implements no such thing: "stay" is a flat zero floor, there is no continuation value, no irreversibility premium, no volatility, no smooth-pasting. Firms wait at low $p$ only because acting has negative expected value, not because waiting carries option value. $s^{*}$ is where two straight lines cross. Calling this "priced as options" and citing McDonald–Siegel/Dixit–Pindyck is analogy dressed as method.
- **§3 — prose/model mismatch on the hedge.** The text says the hedge "convert[s] a threatened loss into a contingent gain," but $H=p\delta s\kappa - a(\cdot)$ is pure positive-EV upside; the model never prices the loss a state-dependent firm suffers if it *doesn't* buy access. There is no downside in "stay" for high-$s$ firms. The threatened loss the prose leans on is absent from the payoffs.
- **§4 — the dissolved "paradox" is close to a strawman.** A bond sell-off (foreign funds) and a lobbying rush (domestic contractors) are different *actors*; that they move differently needs no model. The genuine content is the threshold and the composition claim, not the dissolution of a contradiction few serious observers hold.
- **§5 — non-ceteris-paribus comparisons.** Regimes differ in four dimensions simultaneously (see Claim grounding). The clean result is the sensitivity tornado; the three-regime contrast is suggestive, not identifying.
- **§7 — the regime map is illustrative, not derived.** Norway, the Gulf, China, Russia, the US are placed by hand-chosen $(\delta,p)$ coordinates; the model does not *find* them there. "A map of the regime space puts Norway at the boundary" reads as derivation but is placement. The paper half-concedes this ("model coordinates rather than country measurements"), yet the rhetoric keeps implying discovery.
- **Genuinely strong:** the unit-of-analysis move (firm indexed by $s$ and $m$ rather than "capital"); the claim that the aggregate sign is a composition artifact; and especially §6's point that a cross-section of access-holding is observationally equivalent under option and capture, so identification must be dynamic (event studies on access vs. the odds of power). That last point is a real, publishable methodological observation. §8 is admirably honest about what the construction cannot do.

## Factual errors
- One bibliographic error: Schedelik et al. (2021) page range (642–659 → 514–526). Confidence high.
- No errors found in the Romanian empirics, the comparative figures, the technical definitions, or the reproduced model numbers. The "option value of waiting" invocation is a conceptual overstatement, not a factual misstatement.

## Prose and insight
**Alive:** the opening ("The claim runs a calculation and hides its unit"), the bond-fund-vs-county-contractor contrast in §1, "one repricing seen from two fractions," and "mistaking composition for conviction" — these earn their place and carry the argument. The voice is controlled and unmannered; it avoids AI-slop. **Dead/decorative:** §7's tour from Norway to the Gulf, which spends a lot of confident prose placing dots the author positioned by hand; and the repeated insistence on "optionality proper" and "the analytical content of the word optionality," which inflate a linear threshold into a discovery. **Genuine contribution:** the option-vs-capture *identification* argument (§6) and the composition-artifact reading of the aggregate (§4). Both are reusable beyond this case. The weakness is that they are wrapped in option-pricing language the model does not earn.

## Top fixes
1. **Stop claiming real-options machinery you don't use.** Either downgrade the framing to "expected value of a contingent claim / threshold decision rule" and cite the real-options literature as *analogy*, or actually build the option value of waiting (a genuine continuation value for "stay" under evolving $p$). Right now the first literature you introduce is the one your model contradicts.
2. **Make §5's regime comparison ceteris paribus.** Hold the population weights fixed across the three regimes, or decompose the access-value differences into a parameter component and a composition component. As written, "discretion is what separates the three" is not supported by runs that also move the population.
3. **Fix the prose/model gap on the hedge (§3).** Either price the downside a high-$s$ firm bears without access (so the hedge really is loss-conversion), or rewrite the prose to say the hedge is positive-EV rent acquisition, not the conversion of a threatened loss.
4. **Reframe §6 honestly.** State plainly that the capture DGP is stipulated (reversibility 0) and only the optionality side is computed; the contribution is the *predicted* magnitude (≈7% headcount, 0.23 value-retained) that an event study could test — not a demonstration that the two DGPs differ, which is definitional.
5. **Report the fragility of the identification numbers.** 0.0678 and 0.2263 depend on the chosen $0.6\to0.25$ drop and one draw. Add a small sweep over the probability shock so the reader sees the range, not a point.
6. **Correct the Schedelik et al. (2021) page range to 514–526**, and soften "the larger share … press and propaganda" to "the largest single share (~48%)."
7. **Re-pitch §7 as explicitly illustrative.** Say the country coordinates are author-placed to show the geometry's reach, and drop phrasing that implies the model locates Norway or the Gulf. Trim the section.
8. **Adjust the abstract's "Solved over a population of 60,000 firms, the model locates a … threshold."** The threshold is analytic; the population yields the shares. Small edit, removes an overstatement in the most-read sentence.
